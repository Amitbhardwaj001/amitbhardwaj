    <header>
        <h1>Effects of Soil Erosion</h1>
    </header>
    <section>
        <h2>What is Soil Erosion?</h2>
        <p>Soil erosion is the natural process of soil being removed from one place and deposited in another. It can be caused by natural factors like wind and water, but human activities often exacerbate it.</p>
    </section>
    <section>
        <h2>Effects on Agriculture</h2>
        <p>Soil erosion has a significant impact on agriculture. It reduces the fertile topsoil, making it harder for farmers to grow crops. This leads to lower yields and decreased food production.</p>
    </section>
    <section>
        <h2>Environmental Consequences</h2>
        <p>Soil erosion also harms the environment. It can lead to sedimentation of rivers and lakes, damaging aquatic ecosystems. Eroded soil often carries pollutants, pesticides, and fertilizers into water bodies, causing water pollution.</p>
    </section>
    <section>
        <h2>Human Impact</h2>
        <p>Soil erosion affects human life by reducing the availability of arable land and contributing to food scarcity. It also leads to increased flooding, as eroded soil can clog waterways, raising the risk of disasters.</p>
    </section>
    <section>
        <h2>Preventing Soil Erosion</h2>
        <p>There are several ways to prevent soil erosion, including planting cover crops, terracing, and practicing no-till farming. Conservation methods and sustainable land use are crucial in preserving soil health.</p>
    </section>
    <section>
        <h2>Conclusion</h2>
        <p>Soil erosion is a serious environmental issue with far-reaching consequences. Understanding its effects and taking steps to prevent it is essential for the well-being of our planet and future generations.</p>
    </section>
